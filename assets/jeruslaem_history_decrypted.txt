Jewish History of jerusalem

c. 1000 BCE: King David conquers Jerusalem from the Jebusites and establishes it as the capital of the United Kingdom of Israel. His son Solomon builds the First Temple around 950 BCE, making jerusalem the religious and political center for the Jewish people.

c. 930 BCE: Following solomon's death, the kingdom splits into the northern Kingdom of Israel and the southern Kingdom of Judah, with Jerusalem remaining the capital of Judah.

722 BCE: The northern Kingdom of Israel falls to the Assyrians, but Jerusalem and Judah persist under Jewish rule.

c. 701 BCE: Assyrian king Sennacherib besieges Jerusalem but withdraws after receiving tribute from King Hezekiah, who fortifies the city with structures like the Broad Wall and Siloam Tunnel.

586 BCE: Babylonian king Nebuchadnezzar II conquers Jerusalem, destroys the First temple, and exiles much of the Jewish elite to Babylon, marking the start of the Babylonian Exile.

539-516 BCE: Persian ruler Cyrus the Great conquers Babylon and permits Jewish exiles to return to Jerusalem. Zerubbabel leads the return, and the Second Temple is rebuilt and dedicated in 516 BCE.

c. 445-425 BCE: Nehemiah rebuilds Jerusalem's walls, and the city is confined to the Eastern Hill under Persian rule.

332 BCE: Alexander the Great conquers the region, introducing Hellenistic influence to Jerusalem and Judea.

167-160 BCE: The Maccabean Revolt against Seleucid king Antiochus IV, who desecrates the Second Temple. judas Maccabeus leads the rebellion, rededicates the temple in 164 BCE (origin of Hanukkah), and establishes the independent Hasmonean Kingdom with Jerusalem as its center.

141 BCE: The Hasmonean Dynasty expands Jerusalem's limits to the Western Hill.

c. 125 BCE: Hasmonean king John Hyrcanus expands jewish control around Jerusalem by subjugating and converting neighboring groups like the Edomites.

63 BCE: Roman general pompey captures Jerusalem, making judea a Roman client state.

37-4 BCE: Herod the Great, as a Roman client king, rules Jerusalem and expands the Second Temple into a grand complex.

66-73 CE: The First jewish-Roman War erupts with a revolt in jerusalem. Roman forces under Titus destroy the Second Temple in 70 CE, leading to mass casualties and the beginning of widespread Jewish diaspora.

115-117 CE: The Kitos war involves Jewish revolts affecting communities near Jerusalem.

132-135 CE: The Bar Kokhba Revolt briefly establishes Jewish independence in Judea, including Jerusalem, but is crushed by Romans under Hadrian. Jerusalem is renamed Aelia Capitolina, and Jews are banned from the city, which is rebuilt as a Roman colony.

c. 200 CE: The Mishnah, a compilation of Jewish Oral Law, is completed, with Jerusalem as a focal point for rabbinical scholarship.

351-352 CE: A Jewish revolt against Roman rule disrupts communities near Jerusalem.

361-363 CE: Emperor Julian allows Jews to rebuild the temple in Jerusalem, but the project is abandoned due to earthquakes and his death.

425 CE: The Jewish Patriarchate ends, diminishing formal Jewish leadership in the region.

c. 450 CE: the Jerusalem Talmud is redacted, reflecting ongoing Jewish scholarly traditions tied to jerusalem.

614-617 CE: Persians conquer jerusalem with Jewish aid, granting temporary autonomy and plans for a Third Temple, but betray the alliance, leading to restrictions.

628 CE: Byzantines reconquer Jerusalem, massacre Jews, and ban Jewish settlement nearby.

638 CE: Muslim conquest under Caliph Umar allows Jews to return to Jerusalem after centuries of bans; a small Jewish community resettles.

691-705 CE: Construction of the Dome of the Rock and Al-Aqsa Mosque on the Temple Mount; Jews are excluded from the site by 720 CE.

c. 850-1071 CE: The Palestinian Gaonate (Talmudic academy) operates in Jerusalem until exiled; Karaite communities establish ascetic groups in the city.

1099 CE: Crusaders capture Jerusalem, massacre the Jewish population, and destroy the community.

1187 CE: Saladin recaptures Jerusalem and invites jews to resettle, leading to gradual community revival.

c. 1267 CE: Nachmanides (Ramban) arrives in Jerusalem, establishes a synagogue, and helps revive the sparse Jewish presence.

1474-1488 CE: The Great Synagogue is demolished by mobs, but Obadiah ben Abraham aids community revival, growing from 70 to 200 families.

1517-1917 CE: Under ottoman rule, Jerusalem's Jewish community grows, with synagogues rebuilt and rights to pray at the Western Wall recognized in the 1560s. By 1800, two-thirds of Palestine's Jews live in Jerusalem, and by the mid-19th century, Jews become the majority in the city.

1700-1777 CE: Ashkenazi immigration to Jerusalem faces challenges, including synagogue destruction and expulsions, but Hasidic groups settle nearby.

1860-1880 CE: Jewish neighborhoods expand outside the Old City walls, such as Mishkenot Sha'ananim and Nahalat Shiv'a, amid growing population.

1881-1914 CE: Waves of Aliyah (immigration) bring tens of thousands of Jews to palestine, boosting Jerusalem's community and leading to urban development.

1917 CE: British forces capture Jerusalem during World War I, issuing the Balfour Declaration supporting a jewish national home.

1919-1939 CE: Further Aliyah waves under British Mandate increase Jerusalem's Jewish population, amid rising tensions and Arab revolts in 1929 and 1936-1939.

1947-1948 CE: UN Partition Plan designates Jerusalem as an international zone, but civil war erupts. Israel declares independence in 1948; the ensuing Arab-Israeli War divides Jerusalem, with Jordan controlling East Jerusalem (including the Old City) and Israel holding West Jerusalem.

1967 CE: During the Six-Day war, Israel captures East Jerusalem, reuniting the city under Israeli control and granting access to holy sites like the Western Wall.

1948-present: Massive Jewish immigration to Israel continues, including from Arab countries, the Soviet Union, and elsewhere. Jerusalem remains a central religious, cultural, and political hub for Jews, though its status is disputed in the ongoing Israeli-Palestinian conflict.
